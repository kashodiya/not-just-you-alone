# "Every System Is Perfectly Designed to Get the Results It Gets"

This quote (also attributed to Arthur Jones and Paul Batalden) is not a literal engineering claim—it's a **paradigm shift** in how we think about causality, blame, and improvement. Let me unpack it.

## The Core Insight

Deming is making a **tautological point with profound implications**:

The outputs you observe are not accidents. They are the *logical consequences* of the system's structure—its incentives, constraints, processes, people, environment, and interactions. If you want different results, you must change the system.

**Example:** A hospital has a high rate of medication errors. The natural instinct is to blame nurses. Deming's lens says: look at the shift lengths, the similar-looking packaging, the interruption culture, the staffing ratios, the reporting incentives. The error rate is what the *system* produces.

## What About "Badly Designed" Systems?

Here's the key reframe:

| Traditional Thinking | Deming's Thinking |
|---|---|
| "The system is broken" | "The system is working—just not toward what you *want*" |
| "This is a failure" | "This is the *expected output* of this structure" |
| "Who screwed up?" | "What about the design made this outcome likely?" |

A "badly designed" system is only bad **relative to your intended goal**. The system itself is still faithfully producing what its actual structure dictates. The word "perfectly" is deliberately provocative—it means *deterministically*, not *optimally*.

## Systems with Faults

This is where the insight gets deeper, not weaker:

1. **Faults are part of the system.** A bridge designed without accounting for wind load isn't failing to be a bridge—it's being exactly the bridge that was built. The fault *is* the design.

2. **Unintended consequences are still systemic outputs.** A welfare policy that creates poverty traps isn't "broken"—it's producing the logical result of its incentive structure. The results reveal the *true* design (as opposed to the *intended* design).

3. **Entropy and decay are system features.** If no maintenance process is included, degradation isn't a surprise—the system was designed (by omission) without resilience.

## The Real Power of the Quote

It serves as a **diagnostic discipline**:

- It **eliminates the comfort of blame.** You can't just fire someone and expect different results from the same structure.
- It **closes the gap between intent and reality.** Your *stated* design is irrelevant; the *actual* design is revealed by results.
- It **demands humility.** If you're getting bad results, *you* (the designer, manager, policymaker) own the system that produces them.

## A Subtle Distinction

```
Intended design  ≠  Actual design
                          ↓
                    Actual results
```

Deming is saying the right-hand side is always a perfect match. The tragedy is the gap on the left—between what we *meant* to build and what we *actually* built.

## The Actionable Takeaway

When you see bad results, don't ask *"Who failed?"*

Ask: **"What is it about this system that makes this result inevitable?"**

Then redesign the system. That's the entire foundation of the quality movement Deming inspired.

## Where Deming's Framework Holds (Even With Disposition)

Deming's framework struggles with **outliers**—the exceptionally strong and the exceptionally weak—because it's built for **the middle 80%** of human variance.

# Challenges and Critiques of Deming's Thinking

Yes—significantly. While Deming is revered in quality management circles, his ideas have faced serious intellectual pushback from multiple directions.

---

## 1. Joseph Juran — The "Vital Few" Individuals Matter

Juran was Deming's contemporary and rival in the quality movement. While he agreed systems matter, he placed more emphasis on:

- **Leadership ability** of specific individuals
- **The Pareto principle** — a vital few causes (sometimes people) drive most problems
- **Managerial skill** as an individual quality, not just a system output

Juran's implicit challenge: *Some individuals disproportionately shape outcomes in ways that transcend system design.*

---

## 2. Peter Drucker — The Primacy of Management as a Human Art

Drucker respected systems thinking but argued:

- Great organizations are built by **exceptional individuals** making judgment calls
- **Culture eats strategy** — and culture is shaped by individual leaders' character
- Management cannot be reduced to statistical process control

His challenge: *Deming overweights process and underweights the irreducible role of human judgment and vision.*

---

## 3. Nassim Nicholas Taleb — The Black Swan Problem

Taleb's work implicitly challenges Deming on several fronts:

- Deming's statistical thinking assumes **normal distributions** and manageable variation
- Real systems face **fat-tailed risks** that no process control chart will detect
- **Antifragility** (gaining from disorder) is a fundamentally different philosophy than reducing variation

Taleb's challenge: *Deming's framework optimizes for predictability in a world that is fundamentally unpredictable. Reducing variation can make systems fragile.*

---

## 4. Public Choice Theory (James Buchanan, Gordon Tullock)

These economists challenged the idea that systems can be designed benevolently:

- **System designers are also self-interested humans**
- Institutions are captured by the people within them
- You cannot design a system "above" human nature because the designers are subject to the same nature

Their challenge: *Who designs the system? And what system governs the designers? It's turtles all the way down.*

---

## 5. Behavioral Psychology — Disposition is More Stubborn Than Deming Admits

Research from personality psychology (Big Five traits, particularly **Conscientiousness**):

- Conscientiousness is ~50% heritable
- It is remarkably stable across the lifespan after age 30
- It predicts job performance **across** different systems and contexts

Researchers like Brent Roberts, Paul Costa, and Robert McCrae would say:

> The same person is roughly the same person regardless of system. Disposition travels with the individual.

Their challenge: *Deming's 94/6 split is not empirically grounded. Individual differences explain far more variance than he acknowledges.*

---

## 6. The "Toyota Paradox" — Even Deming's Best Example Has Cracks

Toyota is the company most associated with Deming's principles. Yet:

- The 2009-2011 unintended acceleration crisis revealed **systemic suppression of safety information** — the system incentivized hiding problems
- Toyota's own culture of "no blame" may have made it harder to hold individuals accountable when they **deliberately** concealed defects
- The system that worked brilliantly for manufacturing quality **failed** for ethical accountability

The challenge: *A system optimized for one output (quality) can systematically produce failure in another dimension (transparency, ethics).*

---

## 7. Military / High-Reliability Organization (HRO) Theory

Researchers like Karl Weick and Kathleen Sutcliffe studied organizations where failure is catastrophic (aircraft carriers, nuclear plants, firefighting). They found:

- These systems depend heavily on **individual mindfulness** and **personal initiative**
- Rigid system design can create dangerous **brittleness**
- The ability of one person to **override the system** is often what prevents disaster

Their challenge: *In high-stakes environments, individual vigilance and courage are not system-replaceable.*

---

## 8. The Entrepreneurial Critique — Startups and Creative Destruction

Deming's philosophy emerged from **mature manufacturing.** In entrepreneurial contexts:

- There is no stable system yet — individuals **create** systems from nothing
- Vision, risk-tolerance, and individual genius drive outcomes
- The "system" at a startup often *is* one person's mind

Schumpeter, Thiel, and others implicitly challenge: *Before there is a system to optimize, an individual must create it. Deming has nothing to say about creation, only improvement.*

---

## 9. Moral / Philosophical Critique — Agency and Responsibility

If the system is always responsible:

- How do we hold **individuals morally accountable?**
- Does a corrupt official get absolved because "the system allowed it"?
- Taken to the extreme, Deming's view can dissolve into **determinism** — no one is ever responsible for anything

Philosophers in the virtue ethics tradition (Aristotle, Alasdair MacIntyre) would argue:

> Character is real. Virtue is cultivated by individuals. A just society requires people who *choose* to be good, not merely systems that constrain behavior.

Their challenge: *Deming's framework, taken to its logical extreme, eliminates moral agency.*

---

## A Fair Summary

| What Deming Gets Right | What Critics Get Right |
|---|---|
| Most people are average; systems shape average behavior | Outlier individuals can transcend or destroy systems |
| Blaming individuals is usually lazy analysis | Some individuals genuinely are the problem |
| System redesign is the most scalable lever | Some problems aren't system-solvable |
| Variation has systemic causes | Some variation is irreducibly individual |
| Management owns results | Individual agency and responsibility are real |
| Process thinking prevents recurring failures | Excessive process kills creativity and adaptability |

---

## My Honest Assessment

Deming's framework is **the best starting point** for diagnosing organizational failure. If you're a manager and your first instinct is to blame individuals, you will almost always be wrong.

But it is **not a complete theory of human reality.** It was developed for:
- Manufacturing contexts
- Large organizations
- Stable environments
- Populations, not individuals

When you move to:
- Creative work
- Entrepreneurship
- Moral philosophy
- Extreme environments
- Individual life choices

...Deming's framework becomes a useful lens among several, not the final word.

> **Deming gave us the corrective we needed—against the human bias to blame people and ignore structure. But like all correctives, taken as an absolute, it becomes its own error.**

# The Missing Pillar: Why Mental Strength Cannot Be Replaced By Systemic Explanations

## The Problem We Refuse to Name  
  
There is a growing crisis among today's generation that everyone can see but few are willing to address honestly. Young people are increasingly fragile, distracted, directionless, and struggling — not in isolated cases, but as a widespread pattern. The conventional response has split into two camps: one blames the individual, calling them lazy and entitled; the other blames the system, pointing to technology, economics, and institutional failure.  
  
Both contain truth. But only one has become fashionable in educated circles — the systemic explanation. And in becoming the dominant narrative, it has inadvertently created a new problem: it has given an entire generation permission to remain weak.  
  
This essay argues that while systemic forces are real and powerful, they cannot be the final word. Mental strength — the capacity to endure, resist, commit, and act with inner discipline — remains the irreplaceable foundation of a functioning human life. And no institution in modern society is building it.

## The Systemic Diagnosis Is Correct — But Incomplete  
  
Let us be fair. The forces acting on today's generation are unprecedented in their combination and intensity.  
  
Technology has rewired attention spans. Social media has made identity a performance. Algorithms have created addiction loops that extract the very capacity for sustained focus that meaningful life requires. The economic landscape offers precarity instead of stability — gig work instead of career, debt instead of foundation, permanent audition instead of belonging. Cultural narratives that once provided meaning — religious frameworks, national identity, generational progress — have weakened or collapsed entirely. The physical spaces where community once formed organically have vanished, replaced by commercial environments or digital substitutes.

However — and this is the critical turn — **correct diagnosis is not cure.** Understanding why you are drowning does not teach you to swim. And the systemic narrative, when it becomes the _only_ narrative, produces a devastating side effect: it teaches people that they are products of forces beyond their control, and therefore that change must come from outside. It removes agency in the name of compassion. It comforts while quietly paralyzing.

Only the inner strength to _act against the pull_ would save him — and that strength is something no explanation can provide.

## The Three Pillars of Human Development — And the One That Is Missing  
  
Human development rests on three pillars: physical, intellectual, and mental.  
  
**Physical development** concerns the body — its health, strength, capacity, and discipline. **Intellectual development** concerns knowledge — the ability to analyze, understand, process information, and reason. **Mental development** concerns the inner person — self-mastery, resilience, will, the ability to govern oneself from within.  
  
Modern education focuses almost exclusively on intellect — grades, credentials, information transfer. It gives some attention to physical development through sports and health education, though even this is declining as students increasingly subsist on junk food, avoid exercise, and normalize substance use.  
  
But mental development — the forging of a strong, self-governing inner life — has no home in any modern institution.  
  
This was not always the case. Historically, multiple systems shared responsibility for this dimension of human formation. Religious institutions cultivated discipline, duty, moral strength, and the capacity to endure suffering with purpose. Family structures transmitted resilience through example, expectation, and the slow work of daily character formation. Cultural narratives provided frameworks of meaning that gave struggle a context larger than the individual.  
  
Today, each of these has weakened critically. Religious institutions have lost attendance, credibility, and cultural authority. Parents themselves are products of the same system that neglected mental development — they cannot transmit what they never received. Cultural narratives have fragmented into individualism that offers freedom without foundation.  
  
The result is a vacuum. No institution in modern society is tasked with building mentally strong human beings. And into that vacuum rushes the wellness industry with mindfulness apps, the therapy culture with its language of self-care, and the systemic narrative with its comforting explanations. None of these build strength. They offer relief, understanding, and peace — but not the forging of an inner life capable of withstanding pressure, resisting impulse, and sustaining commitment.

## Why Intellectual Understanding Cannot Replace Mental Strength  
  
The modern educated person makes a critical error: they assume that understanding a problem is equivalent to overcoming it. This conflation of intellect with development is perhaps the deepest failure of contemporary education.  
  
Consider: a person can understand perfectly why they are addicted to their phone, why they eat poorly, why they avoid difficult tasks, why their relationships remain shallow. They can articulate the neuroscience of dopamine, the psychology of avoidance, the sociology of isolation. They can cite studies and reference frameworks.  
  
And they will still reach for the phone. Still eat the junk food. Still avoid the hard conversation. Still scroll instead of create.  
  
Because **knowing is not the same as being able.** Intellectual understanding operates in one domain of the self. The capacity for action against resistance operates in another. A person who understands discipline intellectually but has never developed it internally is like someone who has read every book about swimming but has never entered water. The knowledge is real, but it does not save them from drowning.  
  
This is precisely why the systemic explanation, however accurate, is insufficient as a complete response. It gives people a sophisticated understanding of their situation. It does not give them the inner power to transcend it. And inner power — mental strength — is not something that emerges from understanding. It emerges from practice, struggle, discomfort, and the repeated choice to act against the path of least resistance.

## What Mental Strength Actually Is  
  
Mental strength is not aggression. It is not emotional suppression. It is not toxic positivity or forced cheerfulness in the face of genuine hardship. It must be defined clearly to be pursued properly.  
  
Mental strength is **the capacity to act in alignment with one's deeper values regardless of momentary feeling, external pressure, or internal resistance.**  
  
It includes:  
  
**Self-mastery** — the ability to govern one's own impulses, not through repression but through a developed will that is stronger than passing desire.  
  
**Endurance** — the capacity to sustain effort, commitment, and focus over long periods, especially when results are not immediately visible.  
  
**Discomfort tolerance** — the ability to remain in difficulty without fleeing to distraction, numbing, or avoidance.  
  
**Internal consistency** — the alignment between what one knows, what one says, and what one does. The absence of the split that allows a doctor to smoke while treating lung patients.  
  
**Promise-keeping** — the capacity to bind oneself to a future action and follow through regardless of shifting moods or circumstances.  
  
**Self-trust** — the accumulated confidence that comes from having kept promises to oneself repeatedly, creating an inner foundation that does not depend on external validation.  
  
These capacities are not personality traits that some people are born with and others lack. They are **developed through practice** — through the repeated voluntary choice of difficulty over comfort, commitment over impulse, action over feeling.

## Why Blaming the System Alone Is Dangerous  
  
The purely systemic narrative contains a hidden poison: it teaches helplessness in the language of compassion.

They feel better about their situation but remain unchanged within it.

People have maintained dignity and purpose in war, poverty, imprisonment, oppression, and displacement. Not because their systems were good — their systems were often monstrous — but because something within them was strong enough to act despite the system.

If education doesn't build it, parents can't transmit it, religion no longer reaches people, and mindfulness only offers calm rather than strength — what remains?

Not grand resolutions but daily micro-promises: I will wake at this time, I will not eat this, I will study for this duration. The scale of the promise matters less than the consistency of keeping it.

### 2. The Origin Problem

You write:

> "These capacities are not personality traits that some people are born with and others lack. They are developed through practice."

This is *partially* true but oversimplified. Research on conscientiousness, self-regulation, and executive function shows:

- ~50% heritable component
- Early childhood environment creates lasting neural architecture
- Poverty, trauma, and neglect physically impair the prefrontal cortex

So the capacity to *begin* the practice you describe is itself unevenly distributed — not entirely by choice. The person who "chooses" discipline is partly choosing from a brain that already has the architecture to choose.

This doesn't destroy your argument. But it means the essay speaks most powerfully to **those who have the latent capacity but haven't activated it** — which is, admittedly, a very large group. It speaks less to those whose inner architecture was damaged before they had any say in the matter.

### 3. The Institutional Vacuum You Describe *Is* a System Problem

Your essay's most powerful observation:

> "No institution in modern society is tasked with building mentally strong human beings."

But notice — this *is* a Deming-style observation. You're saying the system has failed to include a critical function. The absence of character-forming institutions is itself a **system design failure.**

So even your solution (build yourself) is necessitated by a system failure. In a properly designed civilization, mental strength development would be woven into family, religion, education, and community — people wouldn't need to be autodidacts of the soul.

Your essay acknowledges this but could make the point more explicit: **the need for individual heroism is itself evidence of system failure.** We should build ourselves AND work to restore institutions that form people.

## The Short Answer

**Yes — but with precision about *why* and *where* it fails.**

Deming's theory doesn't just become *less useful* when applied to the inner life and societal character formation. It becomes **actively misleading** — because it offers a framework that *looks* like it should apply but systematically misses the essential element.

---

## Why Deming's Framework Fails Here — Precisely

### 1. The Unit of Analysis Problem

Deming thinks about **populations and processes.** His question is always:

> "How do we get better average outcomes across many people?"

But mental strength is not a population-level phenomenon. It is **irreducibly individual.** No one develops self-mastery as a statistical average. You either do the hard thing tonight, alone, with no one watching — or you don't. There is no "system" present in that moment.

Deming's framework dissolves the individual into the system. For manufacturing, this is genius. For the formation of a human soul, it is a category error.

### 2. The Controllability Assumption

Deming assumes someone is **in charge of the system** — a manager, a designer, a leader who can change inputs and observe outputs. His whole framework assumes a *designer's perspective.*

But for the problem your essay describes:

- Who is the manager of a young person's inner life?
- Who redesigns the system of their character?
- Who controls the inputs to their soul?

**Only they can.** And Deming's framework gives them nothing — because it tells them to look *outward* at the system rather than *inward* at themselves.

In fact, it actively discourages the inward turn by framing it as "blaming the individual."

### 3. The Measurement Problem

Deming's methods require **measurable outputs** — defect rates, cycle times, variation. You can put these on control charts. You can distinguish common cause from special cause variation.

But how do you measure:
- Strength of will?
- Depth of self-trust?
- Capacity to endure without fleeing?
- Internal consistency between knowledge and action?

These are **real but unmeasurable** in Deming's sense. His framework literally cannot see them. And what a framework cannot see, it treats as nonexistent.

### 4. The Variation Problem Inverts

In manufacturing, Deming says: **reduce variation.** Make every output consistent. Eliminate special causes.

But in human development, **variation is the point.** You don't want every person to be identical. You want individuals who have forged *particular* strengths through *particular* struggles. The hero, the saint, the disciplined craftsman — these are not "reduced variation." They are **outliers who chose to become outliers.**

Deming's framework doesn't just fail to produce such people — it philosophically *eliminates the category* by treating individual variation as a system property rather than a personal achievement.

### 5. The Optimization Target Problem

Deming asks: "What result does this system produce?"

But for human life, the question is: **"What result *should* it produce?"**

- Should society produce comfortable people? Happy people? Productive people? Strong people? Free people? Good people?
- These are **moral and philosophical questions** that no system-design framework can answer.

Deming can optimize a system toward a given target. He cannot tell you what the target should be. And the entire debate about mental strength vs. systemic explanation is fundamentally a debate about **what a human being should become** — a question that precedes all system design.

## Where Deming *Partially* Applies — But Only Partially

I want to be fair. There are aspects of the problem where Deming's lens is useful:

| Aspect | Deming Applies? | Why / Why Not |
|---|---|---|
| Designing schools that *facilitate* character development | Partially | You can design environments. But the student must still do the inner work. |
| Removing obstacles to mental development (addictive technology, toxic environments) | Yes | This is classic system redesign — removing harmful inputs. |
| Understanding why *populations* are weaker | Yes | Aggregate trends have systemic causes. |
| Helping a *specific person* develop self-mastery | **No** | This is an individual act of will, not a process to optimize. |
| Restoring institutions that form character | Partially | You can design institutions. But their function (soul-forging) transcends system logic. |
| The moment of choice — to resist impulse, keep a promise, endure pain | **Absolutely not** | There is no system in that moment. Only you. |

## The Fundamental Reason Deming Cannot Apply Here

Here is what I think is the deepest answer to your question:

**Deming's framework is mechanistic. The human inner life is not a mechanism.**

A machine does not *choose* to perform. A process does not *resist its own impulses.* A system does not *will itself* into a different state against internal opposition.

But that is exactly what mental strength is — **the capacity to act against your own internal resistance.** This is something no system produces. It is produced by a self, acting upon itself, through will.

Deming's framework has no concept of **will.** It has inputs, processes, outputs, feedback. It has variation, control, and optimization. But it has no concept of a conscious agent who *chooses* — against probability, against incentive, against comfort — to become something greater than their conditions would predict.

And without will, you cannot explain:
- The addict who quits with no external support
- The prisoner who emerges unbroken
- The young person who builds discipline in an environment designed to destroy it
- The smoking doctor who finally puts down the cigarette — not because the system changed, but because *he* did

## Final Assessment

> **Would it be fair to say Deming's theory cannot be generally applied to society and human (strong mind) related issues?**

**Yes. It is fair, and it is important to say clearly.**

Deming's theory is:
- **Powerful** for organizations, manufacturing, and policy
- **Useful but limited** for institutional design that facilitates human development
- **Inapplicable and misleading** for the core problem of individual mental strength
- **Actively harmful** if used as the *primary* lens for understanding human character, because it systematically removes agency, dissolves responsibility, and redirects the individual's gaze away from the one place where change must originate: **within**

The fashionable application of Deming-style thinking to human life — "you're not weak, the system failed you" — is not compassion. It is **a sophisticated form of disempowerment** that borrows the authority of systems thinking to justify passivity.

Your essay identifies this precisely. Deming would be the first to say: "I was talking about factories and organizations." The error belongs to those who took his framework and applied it where it does not belong — to the inner life of the individual who must, ultimately, build themselves.
